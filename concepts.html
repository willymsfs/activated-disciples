The Activated Disciple

A Comprehensive Guide to the Core Concepts

Distilled from the work of Jeff Cavins

Concept 1: Fan vs. Follower

The foundational distinction of the book is understanding the difference between being a "fan" of Jesus and a "follower" (a disciple ). A fan admires from a distance, consuming information without application. A follower engages in a transformative relationship that involves putting faith into practice.

The Fan (The Spectator)

  • Knows the information, loves the idea
  • Faith is a hobby or interest
  • Risks boredom and a false sense of security

The Follower (The Player)

  • Puts knowledge into practice
  • Faith is a transformative lifestyle
  • Experiences joy, purpose, and true relationship

Example: The Photography Enthusiast

Jeff Cavins describes his obsession with photography. He bought all the gear, read every magazine, and could talk like an expert. Yet, he never actually took a picture. He was a fan of photography, not a photographer. Similarly, one can know all about the Faith but never actually live it, remaining a fan of Jesus instead of a follower.

Concept 2: Discipleship as Imitation ("Be Holy")

The blueprint for discipleship is ancient, found in God's command to Israel in Leviticus: "Be holy, for I am holy." The core of discipleship is imitation. We are called to conform our lives to the pattern of God's life, which He reveals through His actions in Scripture and perfectly models in the person of Jesus Christ.

"The Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing; for whatever he does, that the Son does likewise." (John 5:19)

Example: The Story of Ruth

Ruth's pledge to her mother-in-law, Naomi, is a perfect model of a disciple's commitment: "Where you go I will go... your people shall be my people, and your God my God." She chose to leave her old life and imitate a new one, based on a relationship. This single act of faithfulness placed her in the lineage of Jesus, showing that our daily acts of imitation have eternal consequences.

Concept 3: Discipleship is Being Chosen

In our modern mindset, we often think we "find" or "choose" God. The rabbinic culture at the time of Jesus reveals a profound truth: the relationship begins with the master's choice. The rabbi chooses the disciple he believes has the potential to become like him. Jesus, the Divine Rabbi, states this clearly.

"You did not choose me, but I chose you." (John 15:16)

Example: The Rabbi's Invitation

A young man in the first century could not simply decide to follow a great rabbi. He had to be invited. The invitation, "Come, follow me," was a profound affirmation that the master saw potential in the student. This truth counters the modern feeling of rejection; the Creator of the universe has seen you, known you, and chosen you to become like Him.

Concept 4: Taking on the "Yoke" of the Rabbi

The invitation to follow Jesus is always paired with the command, "Take my yoke upon you." A rabbi's "yoke" was his unique worldview, his interpretation of the Torah, and his entire way of life. To take on the yoke was to make a 100% commitment to adopt the rabbi's worldview as your own, governing every aspect of your life, from family and finances to morality and conduct.

The call to follow is a call to take on a new way of seeing and living.

Example: Covenant vs. Political Agreement

We cannot treat the yoke of Christ like a political platform where we agree with some planks but not others. It is a covenant relationship, like a marriage. A spouse cannot be 95% faithful. Likewise, a disciple must be "all in," submitting every area of life—even the difficult ones—to the loving authority of the Divine Rabbi.

Concept 5: The Process and Disciplines of a Disciple

The journey of discipleship is a structured process that requires intentional, daily disciplines. The book maps this journey onto the stages of a relationship (Courtship, Commitment, Communion ) and provides the practical tools needed to grow.

The Key Discipline: Lectio Divina

The ancient practice of Lectio Divina (Divine Reading) is the primary tool for turning Scripture reading into a conversation with God. It involves four steps: Reading (Lectio), Meditation (Meditatio), Prayer (Oratio), and Contemplation (Contemplatio).

Example: Building a Prayer Habit

Like a professional athlete who isolates and perfects one skill at a time, a disciple must build a habit of prayer. This involves four practical choices: 1) Pick a Time, 2) Pick a Place, 3) Pick a Bible you can "live in," and 4) Pick a Plan (like the daily Gospel readings). This structure removes the guesswork and creates the space for a relationship to flourish.

Concept 6: Covered in the Dust of the Rabbi

This powerful image encapsulates the goal of daily discipleship. It means to follow Jesus so closely, so constantly, that the "dust" of His life and teachings covers you. This requires shaping your entire day around Him, creating a rhythm of prayer and presence. The shape of your days becomes the shape of your life.

Example: The Discipline of Kavanah

The rabbis practiced kavanah—living with intention and a constant awareness of God. This is the antidote to a fragmented, distracted life. A practical way to cultivate this is to talk to Jesus out loud throughout the day. This simple act cuts through distractions, heightens awareness of His presence, and fosters a continuous, personal conversation with the Lord.

Concept 7: Sharing the Kerygma

The final step of an activated disciple is mission. Evangelization is not about overwhelming people with Catholic "metalanguage" but about sharing the simple, powerful, core message of the Gospel—the *kerygma*. This message is the power of God for conversion.

The 7 Points of the Kerygma:

  1. God loves you and has a plan for your life.
  2. Sin will destroy you and ruin God's plan.
  3. Christ Jesus died to save you.
  4. Repent and believe the gospel.
  5. Be baptized and receive the Holy Spirit.
  6. Abide in Christ and His body, the Church.
  7. Go make disciples.

Example: The Apostolic Method

In the Book of Acts, the apostles shared the Gospel using a three-part formula. 1) The **Event**: They started with something that got people's attention (a healing, a miracle). For us, our changed life is the event. 2) The **Explanation**: They explained the event by proclaiming the kerygma. 3) The **Exhortation**: They lovingly invited people to take a next step. This is our model for sharing our faith in a natural and effective way.
