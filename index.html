I. Introduction: The Call to a Deeper Faith

This guide begins with a provocative question that frames the entire journey: Are you a fan or a follower? It's a call to move beyond passive belief and into an active, life-altering relationship with Jesus Christ, transforming a faith of information into a faith of transformation.

The journey of discipleship is a direct response to a personal invitation from Jesus.

Chapter 1 Are You a Fan or a Follower?

This chapter establishes the book's central theme: the critical distinction between passively admiring Jesus (being a fan ) and actively participating in His life and mission (being a follower).

Core Concept: The Enthusiast vs. The Practitioner

It's possible to know everything about a subject‚Äîits history, its theory, its gear‚Äîwithout ever actually *doing* it. The author uses his own story of becoming a photography expert who never took photos to illustrate this point. This is the danger of being a "Christian consumer" who accumulates knowledge but sees no life change.

The Fan (The Spectator)

  • Knows the information, admires from a distance.
  • Consumes content (sermons, books).
  • Faith is a hobby or interest.
  • Risks boredom and stagnation.

The Follower (The Player)

  • Puts knowledge into practice.
  • Engages in a dynamic relationship.
  • Faith is a transformative lifestyle.
  • Experiences joy and purpose.
"You see, there is a big difference between being a fan of Jesus and being a follower."

Chapter 2 Imitating God

To understand Christian discipleship, we must look to its Old Testament roots. The call to follow God is not new; it is an ancient invitation to imitate the very life of God Himself.

Core Concept: "Be Holy, for I am Holy"

The blueprint for discipleship is found in the Book of Leviticus, where God calls His people to be holy as He is holy. The foundation of discipleship is **imitation**. But how do you imitate an invisible God? He reveals Himself through His words and deeds, providing concrete examples of how to think, speak, and act. Jesus perfectly models this imitation in the New Testament.

Example: The Story of Ruth

Ruth's pledge to Naomi‚Äî"Where you go I will go... your people shall be my people, and your God my God"‚Äîis a perfect model of a disciple's commitment. She chose to leave her old life and imitate a new one. This act of faithfulness had transgenerational impact, placing her in the lineage of Jesus.

Chapter 3 Who's Choosing Whom?

This chapter reframes our entire understanding of the divine relationship. It tackles a fundamental question: does our journey begin with our choice of God, or His choice of us?

Core Concept: Discipleship is About Being Chosen

In the first-century world, a disciple did not choose a rabbi; the rabbi chose the disciple he believed had the potential to become like him. This makes the invitation, "Come, follow me," a profound affirmation. Jesus, as the Divine Rabbi, makes this clear:

"You did not choose me, but I chose you." (John 15:16)

This is a powerful, affirming truth. In a world filled with rejection, the Creator of the universe has looked at you, known every detail of your life, and has chosen you. He believes you have the potential to become like Him.

Chapter 4 Relationship is a Process

Becoming a disciple is not an instantaneous event but a structured, lifelong process of learning and transformation, rooted in the educational system of the first century.

Core Concept: The Three Phases of Jewish Education

The ancient path to discipleship involved three distinct stages, moving from foundational knowledge to radical commitment.

1

Beit Sefer (House of the Book)

Ages 6-10. Memorizing the Torah, laying a foundation of God's Word.

2

Beit Talmud (House of Instruction)

Ages 10-14. Learning interpretation and application while also learning a trade.

3

Beit Midrash (House of Learning)

Ages 14+. For the "best of the best." Leaving everything to follow a rabbi and become like him.

Core Concept: Taking on the "Yoke"

The invitation to follow was paired with the command, "Take my yoke upon you." A rabbi's "yoke" was his entire worldview. To take his yoke meant to fully adopt his teachings as the blueprint for every aspect of life. It is a covenant relationship requiring 100% commitment.

Chapter 6 The Disciplines of a Disciple

This chapter provides the practical toolkit for daily discipleship. Growth in holiness is not accidental; it is the result of intentional, consistent disciplines.

Core Concept: The Art of Praying with Scripture (Lectio Divina)

The primary discipline is prayer, and the primary method for a deep prayer life is Lectio Divina (Divine Reading). This four-step process transforms reading into a conversation with God.

1. Lectio (Reading)

Read slowly, listening for a word that stands out.

2. Meditatio (Meditation)

Chew on that word and apply it to your life.

3. Oratio (Prayer)

Converse with God about what you've read.

4. Contemplatio (Contemplation)

Rest in His presence, beyond words.

This practice is supported by the foundational habits of picking a consistent time, place, and plan for prayer.

Chapter 7 Covered in the Dust of the Rabbi

This chapter provides a powerful vision for what it looks like to live out discipleship moment by moment, creating a daily rhythm that keeps us intimately close to Jesus.

Core Concept: The Shape of Your Days is the Shape of Your Life

The ancient rabbinic saying, "May you be covered in the dust of your rabbi," meant following so closely that the dust kicked up by his feet covered you. To do this, we must intentionally create a rhythm to our daily life that ensures we encounter Him often. This involves:

  • Living a Sacramental Life: Participating frequently in the real, powerful encounters with grace offered in the sacraments.
  • Following the Liturgical Year: Conforming our lives to the life of Christ through the Church's calendar.
  • Practicing His Presence (Kavanah): Cultivating a constant awareness of God's presence in every moment of the day.

Chapter 8 A Cup of "Keurigma"

The final chapter shifts from internal transformation to external mission. It provides a clear, simple, and powerful blueprint for sharing the faith with others.

Core Concept: Sharing the Kerygma

Evangelization is not about overwhelming people with complex theology. It is about sharing the **Kerygma**‚Äîthe simple, profound, core message of the Gospel. This is the power of God for conversion.

1

God loves you and has a plan.

2

Sin ruins that plan.

3

Christ died to save you.

4

Repent and believe.

5

Be baptized in the Holy Spirit.

6

Abide in the Church.

7

Go make disciples.

The Apostolic Method for Sharing

In the Book of Acts, the apostles shared the Gospel using a three-part formula: 1) **The Event**: Start with something that gets attention (your changed life is the event). 2) **The Explanation**: Explain the event by proclaiming the Kerygma. 3) **The Exhortation**: Lovingly invite the person to take a next step.
